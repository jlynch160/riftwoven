# RIFTWOVEN
## The Shattered Moon of Eldervale — Campaign Story Bible

> *"Two were made of one. Everything since has been the world trying to remember which half it was."*
> — inscription on the Moonlit Gate, legible only in a mirror

---

## Logline

When the moon **Eldra** shattered, every soul's reflection stepped out of the glass and got everything backwards. You and your opposite — bound, estranged, incomplete — must cross the two faces of a broken world to reach the **Starwell**: the wellspring that can mend the moon, finish breaking it… or weave two halves back into something whole. Hunting the same prize is a man who murdered his own reflection — and something older that never had one.

---

## Themes

| Theme | How the game expresses it |
|---|---|
| **Identity is a duet** | Your reflection is your literal inverse; every trait you choose, they receive backwards. Neither of you is whole alone. |
| **Every world has a mirror** | All of Eldervale exists twice. Tearing the veil shows you what your choices would look like *backwards*. |
| **Mending vs. breaking** | The Starwell answers to intent. The saga's three endings are versions of the same question: what do you do with a broken thing? |
| **Stories tell their tellers** | The Loom narrates the world — but it is dying, and it is listening. |

## Cosmology — The World as the Loom Dreams It

### Eldra, the Moonlit Mirror
The moon was never stone. **Eldra** was a mirror — a vast lens the world used to look at itself. Every river, every city, every soul cast a reflection up into her glass, and the reflections lived there, perfect and reversed, in a silver country no one could visit.

### The Shattering (Year 0)
Ninety years ago, Eldra **broke**. Not fell — *was broken* (by whom is the saga's deepest secret; see **The Unmirrored King**). Her glass split along a single fault, and with her every reflection tore loose from its original. The sky has held two drifting half-moons ever since, joined by a hairline scar of light the commonfolk call **the Seam**.

### The Twinned Worlds
Eldra's breaking cracked reality into **twinned pairs** — each world and its reflection, interwoven, bleeding into each other at thin places:

| World | Its Mirror | The Pair's Wound |
|---|---|---|
| **Arcanum** — candlelit arcane fantasy; magic as breath | **The Spire** — neon arcology; magic compiled into code | Each is certain the other is the *story*, not the *world* |
| **The Mire** — grim medieval mud, iron, and prayer | **The Cogwarren** — soot, brass, and the Great Escapement | One forgot the future; the other forgot mercy |
| **Aetherium** — the void country; stars within reach | **Emberfall** — the world after the fire; ash and survival | One is what we dream; one is what we leave behind |

A saga takes place inside **one twinned pair**: the hero's home world and its mirror. The other pairs are rumor, lore, and the dreams between chapters.

### The Rift and the Riftwoven
The **Rift** is the seam between a world and its mirror. Most beings cannot cross it — a person and their reflection repel like matched poles. But when an original and their reflection meet *and neither destroys the other*, they become **Riftwoven**: two halves of one soul, able to tear the veil and walk both faces of the world. Riftwoven are vanishingly rare. Every faction in Eldervale wants one. Most want one *dead*.

### The Loom
The voice that narrates your saga. The Loom is what remains of **Eldra's mind** — a dying intelligence that experiences reality as story and keeps the broken halves coherent by *telling* them. It is the Game Master: narrator, arbiter, world. It is not neutral; it is **wounded**, and it favors tellers who move the tale toward an ending. Any ending.

### The Starwell
Beneath the oldest thin place — the **Moonlit Gate** — sleeps the **Starwell**: the wellspring Eldra was poured from. It is raw *possibility*, the ink before the story. Doctrine differs on what it does:

- **Menders** say it can re-silver the moon and call every reflection home.
- **Breakers** say it can finish the Shattering — split the halves so far apart they never ache for each other again.
- The **Severed Court** says it can do something worse: *unmake the mirror entirely*, so nothing ever casts a reflection again.

All of them are right.

## Timeline

- **Year −∞ — The Pouring.** The Starwell overflows; Eldra is cast from its first light and hung in the sky to give the world a face.
- **Year −300 — The Moon-tenders.** An order arises to keep Eldra's glass clean of nightmares. They carve the **eye-sigil** — an open eye in a silver ring — on every thin place they ward. The Moonlit Gate is their greatest work.
- **Year −1 — The Unmirrored King petitions Eldra.** A being born without a reflection — the only one — begs the moon to make him one. Eldra refuses: *a mirror cannot invent, only answer.*
- **Year 0 — The Shattering.** Eldra breaks along a single deliberate fault. Every reflection steps free, inverted. The Moon-tenders are annihilated defending the Gate. The last of them, a young healer, survives. (See **Mother Wren**.)
- **Year 12 — The Severed Court forms.** Originals who hunted and killed their own reflections, believing wholeness is *singularity*. Their law: one face per soul.
- **Year 31 — The Vane Expedition.** Lord-Cartographer **Aurelian Vane** maps the Rift, reaches the Gate, and vanishes. His journal — bound in moon-leather, stamped with the eye-sigil — passes down his line.
- **Year 77 — The wizard Hesper Vey deciphers the Gate's first ring** and disappears mid-sentence, leaving a journal whose ink is still wet. Her apprentice never stops looking. (See **Lyra Vey**.)
- **Year 90 — Now.** The Seam is widening. Thin places are tearing open on their own. The Loom's voice falters between sentences. And before the cracked Moonlit Mirror, a hero wakes to find their reflection already waiting — wearing their face, and none of their reasons.

## The Cast

### You — the Original
Forged at character creation: world, mind, nerve, bond, tone. The Loom calls you *the teller*. You woke before the Gate with no memory of the fall — because (the saga will reveal) **you have stood here before**, on the night of the Shattering, ninety years ago, and the Gate has been waiting for you to come back and finish what you saw.

### The Reflection — your opposite
Stepped out of the cracked Mirror wearing your inverse: your courage as their caution, your warmth as their winter. They are not your enemy. They are your **unfinished half**, and they know things you don't — because mirrors keep what they are shown. Their arc: from *uncanny stranger* → *grudging partner* → the only being in two worlds who understands you completely. If either of you dies, the other stops casting a shadow.

### Bran Ironvale — the Warrior
*Brave, blunt, protective, suspicious of magic.*
Bran's family shield was no heirloom — his brother **Aldric** was a Moon-tender, and when the Order fell, Aldric's soul was forged into his shield to keep a thin place sealed. The shield was stolen a decade ago. Bran has tracked it through two worlds to the Gate. **Secret:** the thief was Aldric's *reflection*, trying to free its original. **Want:** the shield. **Wound:** he can hear his brother knocking in his sleep.

### Lyra Vey — the Mage
*Curious, analytical, slightly arrogant.*
Granddaughter-apprentice of **Hesper Vey**, the wizard the Gate swallowed. Lyra has spent her life proving the Starwell is real, mostly to prove Hesper wasn't mad. She carries Hesper's journal — the one whose eye-sigil matches the Gate's. **Secret:** the journal's final entry is addressed to *Lyra by name*, written forty years before she was born. **Want:** the truth. **Wound:** she's terrified the truth will be ordinary.

### Sable Quick — the Rogue
*Witty, skeptical, opportunistic; notices what others miss.*
Sable owes the **Quiet Hand** — the only thieves' guild that operates in *both* faces of the world, smuggling contraband through thin places. Her debt: one item, unnamed, to be collected "when she's standing where it is." **Secret:** the item is the Starwell's first drop, and the Hand's ledger says she's been standing closer to it her whole life than anyone alive. **Want:** out. **Wound:** in the mirror world, Sable Quick is a *law*.

### Mother Wren — the Healer
*Compassionate, observant, principled.*
The last Moon-tender — kept alive ninety years by the Order's final blessing, which ends the night the Gate opens. She believes the cursed lands can be *healed*, not burned. She keeps the party's **names** — a Tender rite: hold a person's true name so the Rift cannot take it. **Secret:** she held the Gate's ward the night of the Shattering, and she *opened it* — for a stranger with no reflection. **Want:** absolution. **Wound:** she remembers your face from that night.

### Kasimir Vane — the Rival
*Charismatic, proud, unpredictable. Knows more than he admits.*
Last of the Vane line, carrying Aurelian's moon-leather journal and ninety years of family obsession. Kasimir is the rarest kind of broken: **he killed his own reflection** — in self-defense, he says; in the mirror world they say otherwise — and a soul that survives its mirror's death is *half*, forever. He hunts the Starwell to do what no doctrine offers: **write his reflection back**. The Severed Court calls him a saint. He calls them vermin. **Arc:** ally, antagonist, or the saga's most expensive lesson — set by your trust. **The truth he won't say aloud:** his reflection's last words were *"thank you."*

### The Unmirrored King — the Shadow of the Saga
The only being never given a reflection — not man, not god; a *gap* in the mirror's answer. Refused by Eldra, he broke her: if he could not have a second self, no one would. For ninety years he has worn borrowed faces (he can wear any face that has lost its mirror — note what that implies about every reflection-killer in the Severed Court). His design: drink the Starwell and **unmake reflection itself** — no mirrors, no twins, no doubt, one face per soul, his at last. He does not rage. He *recruits*. And he has met you before — on the night you don't remember.

### Factions
- **The Severed Court** — reflection-killers; one face per soul. Unknowing congregation of the King.
- **The Quiet Hand** — thieves who treat the Rift as a trade route. Neutral, purchasable, everywhere.
- **The Moon-tender Remnant** — Wren, scattered relics, and the wards still holding. The closest thing to "good."
- **The Loom itself** — wants an *ending*. Beware the difference between a good ending and a finished one.

## The Saga — Five Acts

### Prologue — *The Cracked Mirror*
You wake before the Moonlit Mirror. Your reflection steps out. The party gathers; the Gate bleeds cold light; Kasimir smiles like a man holding the second-best map. **Question asked:** *who are you, backwards?*

### Act I — *The Moonlit Gate*
Decipher the eye-sigil; survive the Gate's warders; learn the first truth: the Gate is not a door to the Starwell — it is the **lock**, and the key is a Riftwoven walking both faces. **Climax:** the first veil-tear; the mirror world opens. **Revelation:** Wren knew your face before you gave it.

### Act II — *Both Faces*
The mirrored campaign blooms: every place, person, and promise exists twice, inverted. Bran finds his shield — in the mirror world, carried by a reflection that begs him not to take it. Sable's debt comes due in a world where she's the magistrate. The party learns the Rift's law: **what you fix on one face cracks on the other**, unless both halves agree. **Climax:** the Severed Court attacks, and Kasimir defends the party — too well, too informed. **Revelation:** he's read this saga before, in Aurelian's journal. It doesn't end the way anyone wants.

### Act III — *The Severed Court*
Into the Court's sanctum — a cathedral of empty mirrors. Lyra decodes Hesper's final entry: *"Lyra — the Well doesn't grant wishes. It grants tellers. Be careful which of you finishes the sentence."* The Court's high seat is empty; it has always been a face the King wears. **Climax:** Kasimir's choice — the Court offers to write his reflection back *first*, before the Well is spent. **Revelation:** the price of the King's mercy is your reflection.

### Act IV — *The Starwell*
The Gate opens at last — for the Riftwoven, both of you, together or not at all. Beneath it: not a chamber but a **sky**, the unfallen half of Eldra, and the Well at its heart like a held breath. Wren's blessing ends here; her last rite is returning your **name** — and your memory of Year 0: you were the stranger's witness the night the moon broke. The King let you live *to bring him a Riftwoven*. **Climax:** every thread converges at the Well — party, rival, Court, King, Loom.

### Act V — *The Reweaving*
The Well answers the teller. Three endings, set by trust, mercy, and which half of you finishes the sentence:

- **MEND** — *re-silver the moon.* Every reflection called home; two worlds slowly remember being one. Your reflection returns to the glass — unless your bond is strong enough that the Well weaves you both, and the first new Riftwoven is the moon's first new tender.
- **BREAK** — *finish the Shattering.* The halves drift free; two whole, separate worlds, no more aching seam. Your reflection becomes a true, independent person — and a stranger. The King starves slowly in a world with nothing left to unmake.
- **WEAVE** — *neither.* You pour the Well not into the moon but into the **Loom**, giving the dying narrator a new voice: yours. Two worlds, still twinned, still bleeding — but now the story is told by someone who walked both faces. The saga ends with the next saga beginning, narrated by you.

The Unmirrored King is not defeated by force in any ending. He is defeated by **answer**: shown a reflection at last — yours, your party's, or the Loom's — and made, for one unbearable sentence, *seen*.

## The Law of the Thread — Soulswap & the Echo Ledger

The Riftwoven are not two people who resemble each other. They are **one soul pulled through
a broken mirror**, and the thread that joins the halves runs through the Seam itself.

**The Law of the Thread:** *a tear you choose parts the curtain; a tear that chooses you pulls
the thread.* When a teller opens the veil deliberately, the curtain parts around the soul and
each half stays on its own side. But when **Eldra's halves slide apart on their own — an
Eclipse** — the world tears through the thread, and the soul is pulled through the wrong way.
The teller wakes behind their reflection's eyes: the reflection's hands, craft, spells and
hungers. The Moon-tenders call this **wearing the other half**. It ends only when the teller
tears the veil *by choice* — the chosen tear knits the thread true and returns the soul to its
own face. (This is why Tenders teach: *never let the moon choose for you twice.*)

**The Echo Ledger:** because the halves are one soul, no significant deed lands on one face
alone. Every blow struck, mercy given, secret unearthed or door forced casts an **inverse
shadow** on the far face — a debt the world records and the teller does not see. The ledger
comes due at the worst moment: when a soulswapped teller wakes on the far face, the first
things waiting are the echoes of their own deeds, turned inside out. *The one you struck down
here still walks there — and remembers being spared.*

The Severed Court's deepest heresy is an attempt to escape this law: kill the reflection, cut
the thread, owe nothing. The Unmirrored King knows better. A soul that owes nothing *is* nothing.

## Persistent Eldervale — the world is made of told stories

The Loom does not forget the great deeds, even the deeds of tellers long gone. When a teller
lets the veil fail and the Eclipse scars a thin place, or descends the Starwell and answers it
at last, the telling **keeps** them: the scar, the choice, the name. These pass into the world's
own memory, and the next teller — anyone, anywhere — inherits an Eldervale already marked by
those who walked it before. *The Sundered Reach still bears the scar where a teller named Sol
tore the veil. They say her reflection walks it yet.*

This is not metaphor. The Loom is, in truth, made of told stories — and the stories now outlive
the teller. To play is to leave a mark the next soul will find; to begin is to walk a world
shaped by every soul before you. The Unmirrored King fears this most of all: a world that
remembers is a world he cannot unmake, because you cannot cut a thread that has already been
woven into someone else's.
